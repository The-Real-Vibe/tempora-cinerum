Tempora Cinerum

Viaticum

n. — provision for a journey; the rite given to one about to depart.

scroll ↓

I

Nocturne

a piece for the dark hours; music of the night watch, of the quiet held before any light comes.

The birds weren't singing right. The few that called in the dark were off, the sparrows pitched wrong, the crows a beat too slow. He wondered if he'd finally cracked. Months on the border would do that to a person.

Souta hadn't gotten more than four hours of sleep per night in weeks. Too many shinobi were pulled for "remedial training." Nobody really knew what that meant. They only knew the ones pulled never came back. He stopped asking after the third disappearance. Word from leadership had gone strange that season, border skirmishes apparently becoming increasingly common. Whatever the reason, Kasai Ridge didn't have the men to stand all its watches.

He unzipped his tent flaps and the cold came in off the mountain. It was chilly in the dark before dawn, the cooler season's mist taking its first hold of the forest floor. Sparse figures moved amongst a cluster of tents, their low voices blending with the crackle of the first cooking fires. The scent of sizzling bacon and charred pine hung thick in the unlit air.

Souta pressed onward, pausing briefly at a small cascade trickling down the cliffside. One slip on the wet stone, unseen in the dark, would send him tumbling to his death. Kneeling, he cupped his hands to catch the cool, clear water and filled his canteen. Souta thumbed a kunai, its chill biting into his skin. He remembered the one Aki had tried to carve her name into, once. She'd cried when he told her he was leaving. He hadn't known what to say then. He still didn't.

A twig snapped.

An eerie silence fell over the forest. Souta's grip on his kunai tightened as his eyes strained through the black underbrush, searching, looking for anything out of place. Then, just beyond a squat bush: a faint rustle. Inch by inch, he crept closer, every sense on edge. With a sudden burst, he lunged forward, slicing through the foliage in one clean arc.

For a moment, nothing. Then, a low snarl. A fox broke from the bush, a grey shape against the dark, and was gone into the trees.

Just a fox. He let out a breath, a faint chuckle escaping him. Chunin of the Leaf, startled by something half his size. He slid the kunai away but didn't quite relax. Something about the silence still felt off. He lingered on the shrubbery, listening. No birds. No movement. Just stillness.

The murmurs of the outpost grew stronger again. Souta slid the kunai back into his utility belt.

He trudged up the ridge path, boots sinking into damp, rocky soil. As he neared the outpost gate, the first grey of dawn had thinned the mist into threads of silver, darting between wooden walls and guard towers.

II

Requiem

a mass for the repose of the dead; any rite or remembrance offered for those who have passed.

The East Gate's lamp was still burning when Souta came up the ridge path.

He'd seen it from below, a pale smear through the mist. Thought nothing of it until he was close enough to hear it ticking against the glass, the flame going in and out with the draft off the slope. The oil was nearly gone. Someone had lit it at dusk and not come back. He stood under it for a moment, then went to find the other guard.

Renjiro was already at the post.

"You're late," he said.

"Two minutes."

Souta took his place and unclipped his kit. Eight kunai; he thumbed each blade in turn, the tip reading the edge for what a glance wouldn't catch. Four tags. Two soldier pills, the paper on one gone soft with the ridge damp. He pressed the seal and put it back. Twelve shuriken, wire, smoke bomb. The field bandage he kept meaning to replace. He clipped the kit shut.

Renjiro watched the thirty meters of road before the tree line took it. Eleven days on the post together, and Souta had the small things by now, the way you got them off any man you stood beside long enough. Renjiro sharpened left-handed and threw right. He hummed when he thought the wind covered it, three notes, the same three, never the fourth. On the fourth night he had taken a creased photograph from inside his jacket and held it out without a word. A girl, six or so, a gap where a tooth should have been. Then he'd put it away and not spoken of her, and Souta had not asked. Some things a man set down once where you could see them, and then left alone.

The mist sat low between the pines. The camp was waking behind them; woodsmoke, a cook pot, voices that didn't carry far in the cold. Souta's eyes felt packed with sand. He pressed his palm against one eye socket and held it there, and when he took it away the world went briefly bright before settling back to grey.

Hana's team came back from the first sweep as the mist began to clear. Four of them out of the tree line in a single file: loose, quiet, unhurried. It was near lazy for a sweep that found nothing. She was at the front. There was a cut on her neck that hadn't been there yesterday, small, already scabbed. She was talking to the duty officer before Souta had fully registered it, her hand moving in short, clipped gestures. Daisuke behind her, then Shiori, who nodded at Souta the way he always did. The nod that meant nothing.

Everything was fine.

Souta waited.

The fourth man came out of the mist at a walk, hands loose at his sides. He'd gone out with Hana's three. Souta lifted his chin to wave him on. The man came up onto the boards, close, and Souta's eyes went down on their own.

His sandals were dry.

Souta let him through. He kept his hand on the post and looked at the tree line, the grey where four people had just been. The lamp gave one last tick and went out. He stood in the new quiet and let his mind go where it went: the road home, the second checkpoint, the thirty seconds of trail past it where the forest broke and you could see open sky before you'd cleared the canopy. Light through the last of the pines. He'd been keeping that image for eight weeks. Twelve more days.

The assembly bell rang. He pushed off the post and went down into the yard.

The cook fires were lower now, the morning rush thinning. Souta took his bowl from the line, rice and pickled radish and salt-fish warm from the pan, and found the patch near the well where the sun had broken through. He stood in it and closed his eyes.

Just that. The warmth on his face. The food in his hands, the smell of it. The yard moving on without him. He'd claimed this spot in the third week and come back to it every morning since. On the bad mornings it was the thing he returned to. Five minutes of sun.

He opened his eyes.

Hana was at the roster board across the yard, one finger moving down the posted names. Her ration sat on the ledge beside her, untouched. She reached the bottom of the list and started again from the top. He watched her do it twice. He knew the difference between how she looked when she was reading and how she looked when she was counting. She was counting.

He took a bite of the salt-fish and looked for Renjiro.

He was at the far end of the yard, back to the assembly, pressing something into the lining of his jacket. Flat, thin. The shape of something folded once. He smoothed his palm down the front and walked toward the duty roster without turning around.

Souta finished the rice. He told himself what he told himself: men kept things. A man was allowed his pockets. He finished the radish and let the sun do its work and listened to Kenji run the card game, the same points in the same order, losing the way he lost every morning. Souta had shown him a cleaner line on the third day. Kenji had thanked him and gone on playing it wrong, happily, and Souta had let it lie. You didn't take a man's game off him over breakfast. Two men off the western post compared blisters on the barracks step. The duty officer started the afternoon rotation, name after name, the same flat voice that had read every rotation since Souta arrived, the weather and the duty and the names all in the one tone.

He was crossing toward the equipment shed when he stopped.

The training pike was propped against the wall the way it always was, except the shaft had snapped clean at the grip, and the wood inside the break was pale, no grey to it, no weather, which meant it hadn't gone in the rain or the week before but sometime in the night, and someone had stood the two halves back up at the angle the whole pike had always kept.

He looked at the yard around it. Kenji still losing his game. The two from the western post bent over their blisters. The duty officer reading down the rotation. Then he looked at the far end of the yard where Hana stood with her back to the assembly, ration untouched, facing north. The direction her team had come from.

He filed it. Maintenance report, east watchtower, pike shaft compromised. He would write it after second rotation. Put it in the proper box. Someone with more information than him would determine what it meant. That was how it worked. That was how it had always worked, and he had eight weeks of evidence that it worked, eight weeks of mornings where he had filed things and the world had continued and his twelve days were still there waiting for him.

He stood there a moment longer.

He was going to find Aki when he got back. He was going to tell her about the light through the canvas in the hour before dawn, the color of it. Not quite gold. Something thinner than gold. He'd been noticing it, saving it, because she was the only person he knew who would understand why it was worth saving. He'd had nothing to say when he left. He had eight weeks of things now.

Twelve more days wasn't very long.

He picked up his bowl from the sill and went back to the line to return it. Halfway there, he realized he'd already eaten.

He stood there a moment. Then he set the bowl down and went to write the maintenance report.

III

Aubade

a song of daybreak; given to the parting of lovers at first light, sung as the dark ends and one of them must go.

The birds fell silent. All of them, the whole ridge at once, the morning gone soundless between one step and the next.

He was already turning. It was too late the moment the lamp guttered before dawn and he stood beneath it and made nothing of it.

He got the chipped blade up. Aki's, her name a scrawl on the flat of it. Steel met steel, the shock running up his arm into his teeth, and the throw had come from his own post, from the place a man was meant to be standing beside him, so he knew before the turn was finished. He finished it anyway.

Renjiro stood inside his guard, near enough to embrace. Whatever had lived in his face on the fourth night, the man who held a photograph out into the dark and asked for nothing back, had left him as if it never existed. He drove the second kunai up under Souta's ribs and put his weight behind it, the effort of a man leaning on a door that won't give.

In truth, it slid in without struggle. No effort to it at all. His own body gave around the steel and took it, and took it, and went on taking it.

A strangled sound escaped his cold, dry lips.

Then the drills took him, older than thought, older than the friend bleeding in front of him, and he dragged the chipped blade across and felt it open something on Renjiro's arm. Renjiro gave the ground back at a walking pace, unhurried.

Hana came off the roster board and did not come toward him.

The man with the dry sandals crossed the open ground at his own slow pace, taking his time even now, hands already folding into a shape, and Kenji was on his feet with his cards still gripped in one fist and his mouth open, and Souta had a single clear thought, that Kenji didn't know, and then the ground came alive.

It climbed his legs and set hard as the stone it had been a breath ago. He pulled against it and got back nothing. It had his knees, then his thighs, and he was planted to the waist in the dirt of his own outpost with his friend's knife in his gut. The shuriken arrived almost gently, one in the shoulder, one low in the side, and he felt them land and then stopped feeling them as the feeling drew back from his edges toward somewhere deeper in. His hand fell open. Aki's blade dropped into the dirt a finger's reach away. He reached for it. His fingers closed on dirt.

The earth took him.

There is no seeing a thing that comes from under you. One moment he was laying down and the next the ground drove up through him, the stone answering a hand that knew this country's bones, and something gave inside him, deep, a sound he felt more than heard, and the sky tipped down and went grey across his eyes.

He was on his back. His legs had gone somewhere he couldn't follow them to. The grey lowered itself onto him and he let it.

And he waited for the light.

The waiting wasn't a choice. Eight weeks had cut the groove too deep to climb out of now, and so he lay opened on the risen stone and waited for the seam to come into the cloud, for the sun to thin through it the way it thinned through the canvas in the hour before the others woke, the color he'd been keeping all this while to carry home and set in front of her.

The cloud held.

It hung there entire, grey from one edge of him to the other, the sky finally doing the one thing he had never in eight weeks of watching seen it do, which was nothing, which was stay shut, and still he waited, because waiting was the last part of him left running and it had not been told how to stop.

They came up in him then, not as thoughts but as reaches.

His hand lifting to her door, and the door still closed.

The light he'd have raised to show her, the words barely out of him, this, look what I —

Out past the second checkpoint, the thirty seconds of trail where the trees broke and the sky just stood there, and he'd meant to walk her there some evening and say nothing, only point, and let her see it the way he'd seen it every morning until it stopped being a count of days and turned into her, into the whole reason, and he'd —

He never asked what the fourth note was. Renjiro hummed three of them under the wind and stopped, every time, and in eleven days at his shoulder Souta never once thought to ask.

Aki in the doorway the day he left, crying, him with nothing in his mouth to give her then and a whole season gathered up since to make it right, ready now, all of it, right now —

His mouth was full of blood.

Twelve days. He had the number still, sitting clean in him and meaning nothing, and he held onto it anyway.

Somewhere above him, or behind him, a place he could no longer fix, the duty officer read on. Name after name in the one flat voice, no slower for any of it, reaching a name and passing it and reaching the next, and he could not tell whose had just gone by.

The grey stayed grey.

The fires burned themselves down. Kenji's cards lay face-down in the dirt where he'd been standing. The mist came back up off the slope the way it did every morning, filling the low ground, threading the stakes and the towers, and the ridge eased into the shape of any other morning, the one thing it had ever truly been good at. The name for what happened here would come later, from somewhere else, and by the time it came there would be no one left on the ridge to answer to it.
